Abstract. The ﬂeeting nature of existence, or ephemerality, is a power-
ful force in social media, encouraging users to embrace vulnerability and
express themselves more freely. Our research introduces Ephora, a plat-
form that blends the concepts of "ephemeral" and "aura" "Ephemeral"
speaks to the transient beauty of each moment, emphasizing the short-
lived quality of posts and engagement, while "aura" captures the distinct
energy, vibe, and individuality that every user contributes.
Ephora features innovative tools like single-view stories and timed posts
that automatically disappear, designed to cultivate trust, protect user
privacy, and minimize online anxieties, fostering a secure space for shar-
ing. By encouraging users to present their true selves, Ephora aims to
connect individuals genuinely and build meaningful relationships in a
safe and supportive environment. This study explores how Ephora har-
nesses ephemerality to drive authenticity and privacy, potentially re-
deﬁning social media interactions towards a more transformative and
human-focused landscape.

2 Related Work
Ephemerality in social media has gained signiﬁcant attention as platforms in-
creasingly incorporate transient content-sharing functionalities. The concept of
ephemerality originates from the Greek word ephemeros, meaning "lasting only
for a day." [6] In digital contexts, ephemerality refers to information that is
designed to disappear after a set period, contrasting with the traditional ap-
proach of data persistence. Researchers have explored its potential as a solution
to privacy concerns and its implications for user engagement, authenticity, and
emotional well-being.[4]
2.1 Ephemerality and Privacy
One of the most signiﬁcant advantages of ephemeral content is its ability to
address privacy concerns. Privacy calculus theory explains how users evaluate
the risks and beneﬁts of sharing information online[4]. Features like Snapchat’s
disappearing messages and WeChat Moments’ "Visible to Friends" setting help
mitigate perceived privacy risks by giving users control over who sees their con-
tent and for how long[4]. Studies have shown that perceived ephemerality posi-
tively inﬂuences self-disclosure intentions, as users feel safer sharing sensitive or
casual content without the fear of long-term repercussions[4].
2.2 Behavioral and Psychological Impacts
Ephemerality has also been shown to aﬀect user behavior and emotional re-
sponses. It encourages more casual, spontaneous interactions and reduces cog-
nitive burden by eliminating the need for users to curate permanent, idealized

content[6]. However, the transient nature of ephemeral content can evoke "loss
perceptions," such as a diminished sense of control or relevance, which may
deter active participation[6]. Social comparison theory further highlights how
ephemeral stories can sometimes exacerbate body image concerns and negatively
impact self-esteem, particularly among female users [2].
2.3 Spontaneous and Ephemeral Social Networks (SESNs)
Emerging platforms have extended ephemerality into peer-to-peer architectures,
introducing Spontaneous and Ephemeral Social Networks (SESNs)[5]. These net-
works, unlike centralized platforms like Facebook, enable users to interact within
distributed systems, allowing for enhanced privacy and data ownership[5]. SESNs
have been applied to organize events, such as marathons, where real-time updates
and ephemeral interactions create a dynamic and temporary social space[5].
2.4 Engagement Metrics and Content Variety
Ephemeral content encourages users to share a broader range of content, from
mundane daily activities to sensitive personal moments, fostering a more authen-
tic online environment[6]. Features like Snapchat’s disappearing posts require
minimal cognitive eﬀort, enabling users to focus on "in-the-moment" sharing
rather than the long-term consequences of their posts. However, receiving and
engaging with ephemeral content often requires higher attention, as its limited
availability creates a sense of urgency[1].
2.5 Dual Nature of Ephemerality
Despite its advantages, ephemerality introduces a dual nature. On the one hand,
it promotes self-disclosure, spontaneity, and trust. However, it can lead to frus-
tration, cognitive burden, and reduced relevance for users who desire more con-
trol over their content[6]. For example, while Snapchat allows users to specify
how long their content remains visible, overly short time frames can deter users
who feel that the content disappears too quickly to serve its intended purpose[6].
By building on these insights, this research aims to further explore the psy-
chological and behavioral implications of ephemerality. It seeks to bridge the gap
in understanding how platforms can eﬀectively leverage ephemerality to balance
user engagement, privacy, and emotional well-being.

Results
Ephemerality appears as a main drive for sharing intentions as shown, for in-
stance, by responses such as "You only share these things because they are gone
within a couple of seconds" and "Rather than beautiful pictures, you send silly
ones from your day-to-day life so that no one can store them, carry them on their
device, and later use them elsewhere"[6]. With this functionality available, users
are in a position to share a wider array of content, from the most banal aspects
of everyday life to very sensitive topics such as selﬁes, drinking, or embarrass-
ing pictures[6]. It allows users to avoid aﬀectation, irrelevance, and the burdens

of social curation, freeing them to post as if they were having a face-to-face
conversation with a friend. Ephemerality greatly impacts the overall frequency,
breadth, and depth of information that users will share[6] .
Despite these advantages, ephemerality can sometimes be a nuisance for
users who want their content to stay accessible for longer periods. For instance,
Snapchat’s setting of the visibility of content for as short a period as one sec-
ond can be considered by some as too ephemeral. Furthermore, ephemerality
can make applications less relevant to users who would like to retain important
conversations or screenshots for later use[6]. This is what is referred to as loss
perception. As much as the ephemerality makes users more in control of the
information ﬂow, its time-limited shared content availability can also create a
sense of loss, that might inhibit user contributions.
Our model provides insights for social media platform providers in imple-
menting functions inspired by ephemerality. Perceived ephemerality positively
correlates with self-disclosure intentions, as users feel more comfortable sharing
information when they believe it will not remain permanently accessible[4]. Fea-
tures such as the “Visible to Friends” setting on WeChat Moments exemplify how
controlling visibility duration can enhance users’ sense of privacy and encourage
more open sharing[4]. This setting reduces concerns about privacy severity and
intrusion, thereby fostering healthier online sharing environments.
Ephemeral messaging fosters greater interpersonal closeness in social inter-
actions that evoke mild anxiety about what to say by reducing overthinking.
Being “in the moment” through ephemeral messaging allows users to rely less on
past information, thereby mitigating the sunk cost fallacy[3]. This focus on the
present enables individuals to become less inﬂuenced by past investments and
more attuned to present outcomes.
However, the ephemeral nature of stories has mixed impacts on users’ men-
tal well-being. For instance, users who perceive stories as ephemeral are more
likely to engage in social comparison and report higher body image concerns[2].
Social comparison negatively impacts self-esteem and heightens body image con-
cerns, with females showing greater susceptibility due to gender-based diﬀerences
and usage patterns[2]. The ﬂeeting nature of snaps can elicit both excitement
and frustration, particularly when content is lost[2]. Sending snaps requires low
cognitive eﬀort, whereas receiving them demands high attention[2]. In contrast,
stories necessitate more cognitive eﬀort from senders, while receivers engage with
them with relatively less attention.
Discussions
Ephora researched ephemerality and its potential impact on active user engage-
ment, studying both the positive and negative sides by addressing information
perception and loss perception, hence underlining the duality of ephemerality.
This study emphasizes how perceived ephemerality plays an important role in
shaping users’ self-disclosure intentions on social media, calling for features that
enhance user control and reduce privacy concerns[4]. Not only does introducing
